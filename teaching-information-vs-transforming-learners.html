The Difference Between Teaching Information and Transforming Learners

Most courses inform. The best courses transform. Here's what separates them — and why it matters more than anything else you'll do as a course creator.

By Engaging Courses  ·  July 2025

Imagine two cooking classes. In the first, the instructor walks you through a recipe step by step, explaining each technique as they go. You take notes. You follow along. By the end, you understand what they did and why.

In the second class, you cook. You make mistakes. The instructor shows you how to recover from them. By the end, you have a finished dish — and the muscle memory to make it again without the recipe.

Both classes covered the same information. Only one of them transformed you into someone who can actually cook.

This distinction — between courses that inform and courses that transform — is the most important concept in online course design. And it's the one that almost nobody talks about.

What "teaching information" actually looks like

Information-based courses are easy to recognize because they're structured around content rather than capability. They answer the question "what do I want to cover?" instead of "what do I want my learner to be able to do?"

They tend to have long video lectures, lots of slides, comprehensive coverage of a topic, and very little that requires the learner to actually produce anything. The implicit promise is: consume this content and you'll be better off.

That promise is almost never fully kept. Because knowing about something and being able to do something are two entirely different states. And the gap between them doesn't close by watching more videos.

"The gap between knowing and doing doesn't close by watching more videos. It closes by doing — with the right guidance at the right moment."

What transformation actually requires

Transformative courses are built around a different question: what will my learner be able to do — differently, better, or for the first time — when they finish this?

That question forces a different kind of course design. Instead of asking "what should I teach in lesson three?" you ask "what should my learner be able to do after lesson three that they couldn't do before?" Every piece of content, every exercise, every example has to earn its place by moving the learner closer to that capability.

This is what instructional designers call outcome-driven design. And it's not a philosophy — it's a methodology. A set of learnable skills that changes how you build every lesson, every module, every course.

The side-by-side difference

Information-based course Transformation-based course
Structured around topics to cover Structured around outcomes to achieve
Learner watches and listens Learner does, practices, applies
Success = finishing the content Success = demonstrating the skill
Low completion rates Higher completion and re-enrollment
Reviews mention "lots of content" Reviews mention "changed how I work"
Hard to justify the price Price justified by results achieved

Why this is harder — and why it's worth it

Designing for transformation is more demanding than designing for coverage. You have to think clearly about what capability you're actually building. You have to design exercises that create genuine skill, not just comprehension checks. You have to sequence your content so each lesson builds on the last in a way that compounds rather than just accumulates.

It takes more intentionality. But the return on that investment is compounding too.

When your courses produce real transformation, learners finish them. They talk about them. They come back for more. They become the kind of testimonial that sells your next course without you having to say a word. A learner who can point to something they now do — that they couldn't do before they took your course — is worth more to your business than a hundred five-star reviews that say "great content."

The good news

Outcome-driven design is a skill. It can be learned. It can be applied to any subject, any audience, any format. You don't need a background in education or instructional design to build transformative courses — you need the right framework and the practice to apply it.
